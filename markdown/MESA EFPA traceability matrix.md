# MESA EFPA Traceability Matrix

This document is the audit/log artifact for the EFPA-to-MESA adaptation. It complements `markdown/MESA EFPA adaptation map.md`, which gives the conceptual overview, and `markdown/MESA EFPA template.md`, which gives the applied review template.

Traceability supports transparency, justification, and reproducibility: each row records what EFPA element was adapted, where it appears in MESA, how it changed, why the change was made, which literature supports it, and whether the adaptation preserves the Part A descriptive / Part B evaluative boundary.

## Adaptation Type Legend

| Adaptation type | Meaning |
| :--- | :--- |
| `preserve` | Keep the EFPA concept with minimal change. |
| `rename` | Keep the concept but rename it for AI benchmark review. |
| `translate` | Convert a human-testing concept into an AI benchmark equivalent. |
| `expand` | Add AI-specific detail while preserving the EFPA function. |
| `split` | Divide one EFPA concept into multiple MESA fields or rating targets. |
| `merge` | Combine related EFPA concepts into one MESA field or rating target. |
| `add` | Add a MESA concept not directly present in EFPA. |
| `omit` | Do not carry forward a human-testing detail because it is not useful for AI benchmark review. |

## Adaptation Decision Log

| Decision ID | Adaptation decision | Reason | Alternatives considered | Source support | Implication for reviewers | Open issue or revision trigger |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| DL-01 | Treat AI benchmarks as measurement instruments. | MESA reviews whether benchmark scores support intended interpretations, not only whether a leaderboard exists. | Treat benchmarks as software artifacts only; treat benchmarks as datasets only; create a separate AI-only review model without EFPA. | EFPA test-review structure; Measuring what Matters construct-validity framing. | Reviewers must document construct, task, metric, score, claim, materials, and reports as parts of one measurement system. | Revise if future MESA work adopts a non-measurement frame for some benchmark classes. |
| DL-02 | Preserve Part A as descriptive-only. | Descriptive evidence must be gathered before evaluative ratings so later judgments can be traced to sources. | Mix evaluative comments into every descriptive field; skip Part A and rate directly. | EFPA Part A / Part B separation; MESA research conduct guidance. | Reviewers record what sources state and mark gaps without assigning ratings in Part A. | Revise if applied pilots show a recurring need for clearly labeled descriptive caveat fields. |
| DL-03 | Implement Part B as evaluative EFPA-style ratings. | The framework needs a defensible evaluative component while staying aligned with EFPA's `n/a` and `0`-`4` logic. | Use BetterBench 0/5/10/15 scoring; use free-text judgments only; average all criteria into one score. | EFPA Part B rating guidance; MESA template design. | Reviewers assign section and criterion ratings only after assembling Part A evidence and rating-specific evidence. | Revise if pilots show that the `0`-`4` anchors are too coarse or too ambiguous for AI benchmark review. |
| DL-04 | Adapt EFPA norms into baselines, comparators, and reference interpretation. | AI benchmarks usually do not have psychometric norm groups, but interpretation depends on chance, human, expert, model, and prior-version reference points. | Omit EFPA norms; treat model leaderboards as norms without qualification; require human norms for all benchmarks. | EFPA Sections 3.5 and 8; BetterBench baseline and floor/ceiling criteria. | Reviewers should record comparator construction and evaluate whether it supports the claimed interpretation. | Revise if future benchmark types develop stable model-norming practices. |
| DL-05 | Add BetterBench lifecycle criteria. | EFPA covers materials and reporting, but AI benchmarks also need explicit lifecycle review for implementation, reproducibility, contamination, usability, documentation, and maintenance. | Keep only EFPA categories; create separate lifecycle appendix disconnected from ratings. | BetterBench lifecycle, documentation, reproducibility, contamination, usability, and maintenance criteria. | Reviewers should inspect access, code, prompts, scoring harnesses, versioning, release rules, and maintenance evidence. | Revise when new benchmark lifecycle standards become more authoritative or precise. |
| DL-06 | Add the Measuring what Matters construct-validity chain. | AI benchmarks can make large claims from narrow tasks and metrics; the chain makes the inference visible and reviewable. | Record construct and metric separately without linking them; evaluate validity only in free text. | Measuring what Matters; EFPA validity logic in Section 10. | Reviewers must identify phenomenon, definition, task operationalization, sampling logic, metric, score interpretation, and claim. | Revise if pilots identify additional links, such as intervention, deployment context, or decision consequence. |
| DL-07 | Add AGI/CHC caution for broad intelligence and agency claims. | Broad intelligence, AGI, agency, or cross-domain claims need breadth and depth evidence rather than isolated performance. | Ignore AGI language as marketing; rate all broad claims under general validity only. | A Definition of AGI; CHC broad/narrow ability logic in project guidance; Measuring what Matters claim proportionality. | Reviewers screen for broad-claim terms and check whether relevant sub-abilities, domains, modalities, tools, memory, planning, and speed are covered or excluded. | Revise when the thesis settles the exact CHC/AGI terminology and source set. |
| DL-08 | Require evidence, missing evidence, reviewer rationale, and interpretation caution for every Part B rating. | Ratings should be auditable and should separate documented support from absent or unclear evidence. | Store evidence only in prose; require evidence only for overall ratings; collapse missing evidence into the numeric score. | EFPA rating discipline; MESA research conduct guidance; Measuring what Matters evidence-based interpretation. | Reviewers cannot enter a bare number; each rating must explain what is known, what is missing, why the score was chosen, and how to interpret it cautiously. | Revise if template usability testing shows the row structure is too heavy for low-risk criteria. |
| DL-09 | Treat missing documentation as an evidence gap, not proof of poor quality. | Absence of local evidence may reflect inaccessible materials, review scope, or publication choices rather than benchmark failure. | Penalize all missing evidence automatically; ignore missing evidence in ratings. | MESA research conduct guidance; EFPA source-grounded review logic. | Reviewers should mark gaps clearly and rate only the evidentiary support available for the intended use. | Revise if a benchmark domain establishes mandatory documentation requirements that justify stronger default penalties. |
| DL-10 | Do not mechanically average ratings. | EFPA-style review depends on reviewer judgment, intended use, critical evidence gaps, and claim scope; averages can hide severe validity or reproducibility problems. | Compute one mean score; weight all sections equally; use pass/fail only. | EFPA final evaluation guidance; MESA summary-rating design. | Reviewers synthesize ratings narratively and identify critical evidence gaps rather than relying on arithmetic. | Revise if future validation produces empirically justified weighting rules for specific benchmark classes. |
